# Delayed Results

## Overview

The delayed quiz was completed approximately two weeks after the initial learning phase. This was the more informative test of the three learning conditions because the immediate quiz showed very high performance overall.

A total of 30 participants completed both quizzes.

## Overall delayed performance

| Quiz | Participants | Mean score | Median score | Full marks |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Quiz 2 (delayed) | 30 | 7.07 / 10 | 7 / 10 | 4 |

Compared with the immediate quiz, overall performance dropped noticeably. On average, participants scored **2.43 marks lower** in the delayed quiz. This shows that forgetting occurred over time and makes the delayed quiz the better measure of whether the different learning conditions produced stronger memory traces.

## Condition-level delayed performance

| Condition | Correct | Total | Accuracy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Image + explanation | 76 | 100 | 76% |
| Explanation only | 68 | 100 | 68% |
| Word only | 68 | 100 | 68% |

The delayed results separated the conditions more clearly than the immediate quiz. The **image + explanation** condition performed best, reaching **76% accuracy**, while both **explanation only** and **word only** reached **68% accuracy**.

## Forgetting across conditions

From Quiz 1 to Quiz 2:

- image + explanation dropped by **21 percentage points**
- explanation only dropped by **26 percentage points**
- word only dropped by **26 percentage points**

This suggests that image-supported mnemonic learning did not eliminate forgetting, but it did appear to reduce it. By contrast, explanation only did not show a delayed advantage over the baseline word-only condition in this dataset.

## Interpretation

The delayed results suggest that the strongest support came from combining a mnemonic explanation with an image rather than using explanation alone or simple word-translation exposure.

This is an important finding for the project because it suggests that **dual-coded support** was more beneficial for retention than verbal support alone. At the same time, the difference was moderate rather than overwhelming, so the result should be interpreted as **promising evidence** rather than a definitive proof.

## Word-level delayed patterns

| Word | Overall delayed accuracy | Strongest condition in Quiz 2 |
|---|---:|---|
| *dom* | 66.7% | Explanation only (80.0%) |
| *kot* | 93.3% | Explanation only / word only tie (100.0%) |
| *okno* | 70.0% | Explanation only (81.8%) |
| *pies* | 46.7% | Word only (60.0%) |
| *ser* | 66.7% | Image + explanation (90.0%) |
| *zima* | 60.0% | Image + explanation / word only tie (70.0%) |
| *sklep* | 63.3% | Image + explanation (80.0%) |
| *stary* | 83.3% | Image + explanation (100.0%) |
| *torba* | 80.0% | Word only (90.0%) |
| *ptak* | 76.7% | Image + explanation (90.0%) |

These word-level patterns show that image support was **not uniformly best for every word**. It was especially helpful for *ser*, *sklep*, *stary*, and *ptak*, but not for *pies* or *torba*. Explanation only was strongest for some items, especially *dom* and *okno*.

This was one of the most informative findings of the study. The key question was not simply whether AI-generated mnemonics worked in general, but **which kinds of mnemonics worked, for which words, and why**.

## Main takeaway

The delayed quiz provided the clearest evidence in the project. The **image + explanation** condition was the strongest overall condition for retention, while **explanation only** did not outperform **word only** in the delayed data.

This supports the conclusion that AI-generated mnemonic imagery can be useful for vocabulary learning, but its value depends heavily on the quality of the individual mnemonic.
